Executive Summary

An Arduino Uno, an HC-SR04 ultrasonic rangefinder, and an SG92 micro servo work together to balance a rolling ball at the center of an 18-inch beam. The beam-angle-to-ball-position plant is an inherently unstable double integrator; it is stabilized entirely through a tuned PID feedback loop running on the Arduino, with no mechanical damping assist. The system met all four target performance specifications (rise time, settling time, overshoot, steady-state error) on physical hardware.

2

Problem Statement

Demonstrate closed-loop control of an open-loop-unstable plant using low-cost, readily available hardware, and prove the design against explicit, measurable performance targets rather than a qualitative "it balances" outcome. Ultrasonic distance sensing is inherently noisy at this range and sample rate, which the control and filtering design must account for without introducing unacceptable lag.

System Architecture

The beam-angle-to-ball-position transfer function was modeled from first principles and reduces to a double integrator, confirming open-loop instability analytically before any hardware was built. The Arduino Uno closes the loop at a fixed sample interval: it reads the HC-SR04 range, converts it to a ball position, computes a PID correction against the setpoint, and writes a corresponding angle to the SG92 servo.

Implementation

Firmware robustness measures were the majority of the implementation effort, since raw ultrasonic readings were too noisy to feed directly into the control loop:

  • 5-sample median filter followed by an exponential moving average on raw ultrasonic readings.
  • ±2 cm deadband to suppress micro-corrections once the ball had settled near setpoint.
  • Integral-reset anti-windup and output clamping to keep the servo command within physical limits.
  • Dedicated lost-ball and proximity-recovery routines for out-of-range sensor conditions.
  • Live serial telemetry streamed to a MATLAB script, plotting ball distance and servo angle in real time for tuning and verification.

Final tuned gains: Kp = 1.3, Ki = 0.034, Kd = 0.4.

7

Testing

Step-response tests were run directly on hardware (not simulation), with ball position and servo angle logged over serial to the MATLAB telemetry script for measurement.

Engineering Decisions

Filtering was chosen over a higher-cost, lower-noise sensor (e.g., a laser rangefinder) to keep the bill of materials low, on the reasoning that a well-tuned median + EMA filter plus deadband could absorb HC-SR04 noise cheaply. This traded a small amount of settling-time margin for a substantially simpler and cheaper sensing stage — the measured results show the margin was not needed.

10

Lessons Learned

Modeling the plant analytically before touching hardware (confirming the double-integrator instability) made the subsequent tuning process far faster than a pure trial-and-error approach would have been — the analytical model correctly predicted that proportional control alone could not stabilize the plant.

11

Future Improvements

  • Replace ultrasonic ranging with a linear resistive position sensor to remove the filtering-induced lag entirely.
  • Add a second axis (2-DOF platform) to extend the demonstration to planar ball balancing.
  • Auto-tune Kp/Ki/Kd via relay feedback instead of manual tuning.
