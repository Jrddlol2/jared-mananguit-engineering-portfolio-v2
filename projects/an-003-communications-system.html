Executive Summary

A complete QAM communications chain was built in MATLAB/Simulink to transmit two independent audio signals over a single shared bandwidth — from resampling and carrier modulation through coherent demodulation and zero-phase reconstruction. Subjective listening tests found no audible loss versus the original source signals on either recovered channel.

3

System Overview

Two audio sources are converted to mono, sample-rate matched, and zero-padded for synchronization, then resampled well above the Nyquist rate of the shared carrier. Quadrature Amplitude Modulation combines both normalized channels onto orthogonal in-phase/quadrature carriers for transmission over a single shared channel. At the receiver, coherent demodulation with synchronized local oscillators separates and recovers each baseband signal, which is then filtered, downsampled, and exported for comparison against the originals.

System Design

Both audio channels are normalized and modulated onto orthogonal in-phase and quadrature carriers so they can share one transmission band without interfering with one another. The carrier frequency was set to 100 kHz; the signal chain resamples to 3× that rate before modulation — comfortably above Nyquist — after early testing at the Nyquist rate itself produced audible aliasing distortion. Coherent demodulation recovers each channel by multiplying against locally generated oscillators synchronized to the transmitter, and a 4th-order Butterworth low-pass filter isolates the recovered baseband signal from the demodulation product.

6

Implementation

  • Mono conversion, sample-rate matching, and zero-padding to synchronize both source channels.
  • Polyphase resampling to 3× the 100 kHz carrier frequency, well above Nyquist.
  • QAM modulation combining the two normalized audio channels onto orthogonal I/Q carriers.
  • Coherent demodulation via synchronized local oscillators to recover each baseband channel.
  • Baseband recovery through a 4th-order Butterworth low-pass filter applied with filtfilt for zero-phase distortion.
  • Downsampling the recovered signals back to their original ~48 kHz rate and exporting as .wav for comparison.
7

Testing

Each recovered channel was exported alongside its original source and compared via subjective A/B listening tests, in both time domain and frequency domain views, to check for perceptible artifacts introduced by the modulation/demodulation chain.

8

Results

Subjective listening tests showed no audible degradation on either recovered channel versus its original source. Resampling at 3× the carrier frequency eliminated the aliasing distortion observed in earlier iterations that resampled at exactly the Nyquist rate, and the zero-phase Butterworth filter avoided the audible phase smearing produced by an early causal single-pass filter design.

Quantitative SNR / EVM measurement — not performed (qualitative listening test only)
9

Lessons Learned

Resampling exactly at the Nyquist rate is not the same as resampling safely above it — the audible aliasing at the Nyquist boundary only became apparent once the signal was actually listened to, not just inspected on a spectrum plot, which reinforced the value of perceptual testing alongside numerical analysis. Zero-phase filtering (filtfilt) also proved necessary rather than optional: a single-pass causal filter's phase distortion was subtle on a plot but audible on playback.
